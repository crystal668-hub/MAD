# Three-dimensional cross-linked Co-MoS<sub>2</sub> catalyst on carbon cloth for efficient hydrogen evolution reaction<sup>â€ </sup>

Yan Xiao, Jing Yao, Tianze Zhang, Xinzhi Ma, Dexin Xu and Hong Gao ðŸ—… \*

MoS<sub>2</sub>-Based materials are promising hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) electrocatalysts. However, their HER activities are restrained by the poor population of HER activated edge centers, the large area exposed HER inert basal planes, and low conductivity. Fixing these problems on one system is an effective strategy, but it remains a challenge due to the harsh synthetic conditions. Herein, cobalt carbonate hydroxide (CoCH) nanosheets were used as the substrate for preparing a three-dimensional self-supported cross-linked (3DSC) Co-MoS<sub>2</sub> nanostructured HER catalyst, which possesses abundant active centers and fast electronic transfer ability. In addition, Co activates the basal-plane sulfur atom in MoS<sub>2</sub> to be the HER reactive center effectively. Benefiting from these advantages, 3DSC Co-MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode integrated on carbon cloth (CC) shows that it can drive the current density of 10 and 100 mA cm<sup>-2</sup> with only 40 and 119 mV overpotentials, respectively, which is superior to other MoS<sub>2</sub>-based HER catalysts reported recently. This research provides a facile strategy for the improvement of efficient HER electrocatalysts.

## Introduction

With the development of human society, the increase in energy consumption has brought about a scarcity of nonrenewable resources, accompanied by environmental problems such as global warming.1-3 Hydrogen energy has broad development prospects due to its cleanliness, renewability, and high gravimetric energy density characteristics.<sup>4,5</sup> Electrochemical water splitting is the first choice for largescale clean hydrogen production among many hydrogen production methods because of its direct and clean fashion.<sup>6-8</sup> Choosing a suitable catalyst can make the process more efficient. However, as the best-performing hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) catalyst, Pt-based is expensive and has small reserves, severely restricting industrial hydrogen production's extensive application. 9,10 Therefore, it is necessary to develop highly efficient and inexpensive electrocatalysts to satisfy universal applications.

In recent years, researchers have been working to find highly efficient, durable non-precious metal electrocatalysts, such as transition metal phosphides, 11 sulfides, 12,13 car-

To develop an advanced MoS<sub>2</sub>-based HER catalyst, it is highly desired to expose many active centers, and create new reactive centers by activating the inactive sites.<sup>25</sup> Based on these considerations, researchers mainly focus on the following three optimization strategies: (1) Boosting the intrinsic catalytic activity of the catalyst. The results of many studies show that the doping of transition metal elements can effectively change the electronic structure to improve the HER activity of the material.<sup>26â€“30</sup> Liu *et al.* constructed a theoretical physical model, where Fe, Co, Cu, and Ni atoms were employed as single dopants. They demonstrated that the doped transition-metal atoms can connect themselves with S atoms, and this S atom is the most active site for hydrogen bonding, thus contributing to the activation of the inert basal planes in MoS<sub>2</sub>.<sup>31</sup> Attanayake's group confirmed the correct-

They found that introducing Co (Ni) in the inert plane of MoS<sub>2</sub> can reduce the  $\Delta G_{\rm H}$  and promote water separation. These advantages endow MoS2 excellent HER activity in the alkaline medium.<sup>32</sup> Therefore, the introduction of transition metals can improve the limitation of inert substrates toward the intrinsic activity of MoS2. (2) Augmenting the number of superior active centers on the edges. The three-dimensional (3D) structure is advantageous for increasing the exposed edge sites, such as nanospheres, nanoclusters, nanotubes, and nanoflowers. Li et al. developed MoS2 nanoflowers with 210-470 nm diameter constructed by small nanosheets. Based on this advantage, Ho's team designed an architectural MoS2 nanotubes array. This strategy maximizes the per unit geometric area, which contributed to the exposure of a large number of active centers on the edges.<sup>34</sup> Therefore, the construction of 3D structures has become the primary strategy to synthesize nanostructures with a high density of the active edges. (3) Establishing easy electron transfer system. The self-supported electrode structure is more attractive than the traditional coated powdery modality because it can provide a facile pathway for electron transfer and a more robust mechanical capability. These advantages make it an ideal electrode with better kinetics and stability.<sup>35</sup> Also, a self-supported electrode system can also afford the integration of various catalytic centers.35,36 For example, Tang's group developed a unique self-supported hierarchical MoS<sub>2</sub>/ CoS<sub>2</sub> nanotubes array, wherein the classical hierarchical and nanotube array structure endowed the electrode with a low Tafel slope of 34 mV dec<sup>-1</sup> and excellent durability of more than 72 hours.<sup>37</sup> Though these strategies have been employed individually or in combination for preparing high efficiency MoS<sub>2</sub>-based HER catalysts, the integration of three approaches in one system has been less reported, which may be due to the harsh synthetic environment. Thus, an electrode that meets the overall needs of excellent catalytic ability, several reactive centers, good electrical conductivity, and a robust structural integrity can be achieved by purposefully adjusting only the morphology.

Herein, a 3D self-supported cross-linked (3DSC) Co-contained  $MoS_2$  (Co- $MoS_2$ ) nanostructured HER catalyst was synthesized on carbon cloth (CC) via a hydrothermal method, followed by an in situ grafting process. Benefiting from the unique structure,  $MoS_2$  nanosheets obtain rivets and uniformly disperse, thus exposing abundant active centers on the edges and resulting in a more suitable environment for ion transfer and bubble release. On the other hand, the unique structure also strengthens the durability and accelerates electron transfer by direct bonding with CC. In addition, the Co element in  $MoS_2$  improves the overall conductivity to provide an easy path for electron transfer and successfully activates the adjacent S sites to respond to HER. As a result, the 3DSC Co- $MoS_2$ -4/CC prepared using the cobalt carbonate hydroxide

(CoCH) nanosheets prepared with 4 mmol reagent as the basis shows the most significantly improved HER response and durability. It needs only 40 and 119 mV overpotential to drive the current density of 10 and 100 mA cm<sup>-2</sup> in 1 M KOH, respectively. In addition, it can drive the current density of 10 mA cm<sup>-2</sup> for 24 hours without any structural changes. This work provides a facile strategy for integrating the exposure of the active centers, conductivity improvement, and durability in one system.

## Experimental

#### Synthesis of the CoCH/CC nanosheet

Before synthesis, CC (WOS1009) was dipped in a 3 M HNO<sub>3</sub> aqueous solution and placed in a Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave, which was treated in an oven at 90 Â°C overnight. All the CoCH precursor nanosheets were collectively referred to as CoCH/CC.  $\text{Co(NO_3)}_2\text{Â·}6\text{H}_2\text{O}$  (1 mmol; 4 mmol; 8 mmol) and urea (5 mmol; 20 mmol; 40 mmol) were dissolved in 25 mL DI water and stirred for 30 min. A 2 Ã— 2 cmÂ² piece of CC and the pink solution were transferred into a 50 mL Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave and treated at 120 Â°C for 360 min. The prepared sample was washed with DI water and dried under vacuum overnight. They were named as CoCH-1/CC, CoCH-4/CC, and CoCH-8/CC, respectively.

#### Synthesis of the Co-MoS<sub>2</sub>/CC composites

All samples introduced with Co elements are collectively referred to as Co-MoS<sub>2</sub>/CC. Firstly,  $(NH_4)_6Mo_7O_{24}\cdot 4H_2O$  (0.6 mmol) and thiourea (18.7 mmol) were dissolved in 25 mL DI water and stirred vigorously for 0.5 h. Subsequently, the mixture and prepared CoCH/CC samples were transferred to a 50 mL Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave. After heating to 200 Â°C and maintaining for 480 min, the autoclave was cooled to room temperature naturally. The resulting product was alternately rinsed, and then dried in a vacuum overnight. Finally, the products were annealed for 90 min at 350 Â°C under a 100 sccm Ar atmosphere with a heating rate of 5 Â°C min<sup>-1</sup> to obtain the Co-MoS<sub>2</sub>-1/CC, Co-MoS<sub>2</sub>-4/CC, and Co-MoS<sub>2</sub>-8/CC samples.

#### **Electrochemical measurements**

All electrochemical tests were performed under a standard three-electrode set up using a VMP3 (France) electrochemical workstation, wherein Hg/HgO (1 M KOH) was used as the reference electrode, and 1 Ã— 1 cm<sup>2</sup> Co-MoS<sub>2</sub>-4/CC and graphite rod were used as the working and counter electrodes, respectively.

Linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) was measured to study the HER performance at the rate of 2 mV s<sup>-1</sup> in 1 M KOH electrolyte. All the measured potentials were converted into reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE) potential using the following formula.

$$E_{(RHE)} = E_{(Hg/HgO)} + 0.098 + 0.059 \times pH$$

The Tafel slopes were acquired from the LSV plots. The stabilities of these catalysts were assessed by carrying out cyclic voltammetry (CV) for 2000 cycles at a scan rate of 100 mV s<sup>-1</sup>. Chronopotentiometry tests were conducted at 10 mA cm<sup>-2</sup> in HER condition. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was carried out at a polarization voltage of -200 mV vs. RHE in 1 M KOH electrolyte in the frequency range from 10 mHz to 100 kHz using the bias voltage of 10 mV. The electrochemical double-layer capacitance  $(C_{dl})$  was calculated from the CV curves. The CV plots were measured in the scan rate range of 5 to 30 mV s<sup>-1</sup> in the non-faradaic potential range of 0.364-0.464 V vs. RHE. The electrochemically active surface area (ECSA) can be obtained by dividing the  $C_{\rm dl}$  by the specific capacitance  $(C_{\rm s})$ ;  $C_{\rm s}$  was assumed to be 0.04 mF cm<sup>-2</sup> in this study.

## Conclusions

**Dalton Transactions** 

In summary, a 3DSC Co-MoS<sub>2</sub>/CC catalyst was designed and synthesized by ingenious nanostructure engineering. Firstly, CoCH nanosheets provide abundant rivet and diffusion sites for MoS<sub>2</sub> nucleation. The rough established structure reduces the adhesion of bubbles and accelerates the movement of electrolytes and bubbles. Secondly, forming the Co-S bonds activates the inert basal planes to improve its catalytic activity and provides a continuous electron transfer path to improve the conductivity of materials. Finally, the unique sheet-crosslinked structure has excellent stability and ensures fast charge transfer by bonding with CC. The significant increase in the active sites and the formation of Co-S bonds lay the foundation for the substantial improvement of the hydrogen evolution performance. The catalyst is superior to most of the nonprecious electrocatalysts reported.
