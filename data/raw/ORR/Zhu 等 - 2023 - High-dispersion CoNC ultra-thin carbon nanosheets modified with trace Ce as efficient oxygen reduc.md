# High-dispersion Co/N/C ultra-thin carbon nanosheets modified with trace Ce as efficient oxygen reduction reaction catalystsâ€ 

Xiaogi Zhu, Shengnan Xie, Xin Fu, Sheng Zhu, Yulin Min, Qunjie Xu and Qiaoxia Li ðŸ•© \*

Efficient precious metal-free oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) electrocatalysts are key to reducing the cost of fuel cells and metal-air batteries. In this study, we synthesized Ce-Co-based N-doped porous carbon nanosheets (Ce-Co/N/C) by introducing a small amount of rare earth element Ce to improve the catalytic activity of Co-based electrocatalysts. Here, folic acid (FA) was used as a metal complex ligand, and Ce-Co/ N/C was synthesized using a solvothermal method with molten salt template-assisted calcination. The resulting Ce-Co/N/C material exhibited superior ORR performance in alkaline media with a high half-wave potential of 0.893 V (vs. RHE), splendid stability and methanol resistance, which are significantly better than those of commercial 20% Pt/C. These favorable chemical properties are due to their advantageous composition and structure. Transmission electron microscopy and N2 adsorption-desorption experiments showed that the catalyst has a large specific surface area and a hierarchical pore structure. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy revealed that the change in Co binding energy after Ce doping indicates the adjustment of the Co electronic structure, which optimizes the adsorption/desorption of oxygen-containing intermediates and greatly improves the catalytic activity for oxygen reduction.

# 1. Introduction

Advanced energy conversion and storage systems are gaining much attention for their significant role in clean energy technologies. Among the new sustainable energy technologies available, fuel cells are considered to be the most suitable energy carriers and one of the energy conversion technologies.<sup>2,3</sup> It was found that the speed of cathodic oxygen reduction has a significant influence on the efficiency (ORR) of fuel cells.<sup>4</sup> Therefore, improving the slow reaction kinetics in the ORR process can help enhance the cell efficiency of fuel cells. The use of appropriate catalysts is the main means to improve this process.<sup>5</sup> Precious metals are mainly used as fuel cell catalysts; however, this has increased the cost of fuel cells dramatically.4 In order to further the use and development of fuel cells on a broad scale, nonprecious metal ORR catalyst research and development have become a top research priority in the field.

Recently, many researchers have been focusing on developing high-efficiency, durable, and low-cost transition metal M-N-C

As a substitute for noble metalbased electrocatalysts, transition metal Fe-N-C has become a promising and competitive candidate because of its high conductivity and activity.9 However, there are many challenges in material preparation due to the high specific surface free energy of the Fe catalyst and its easy migration and agglomeration. Replacing Fe-based catalysts with Co-based catalysts 10 can overcome these problems. Compared to Fe-based catalysts, the activity of Co-based catalysts must be improved further. Structural control and catalyst surface engineering are considered to be the most effective tactics. 11 The total reaction rate in a catalytic process depends on the mass transfer of reaction intermediates and products and the transfer of electrons on the catalyst surface. 12 Different carbon topologies, particularly two-dimensional carbon nanosheets, have a significant amount of specific surface areas, exposing more effective catalytic reactive centers for the associated catalytic reaction. More than that, the layered structure of twodimensional carbon nanosheetscan serve as a transport route for reactants and intermediaries, which is advantageous for catalytic processes.<sup>13</sup> In addition, to prepare high activity catalysts, it is necessary to fundamentally improve the intrinsic activity of active sites. of the catalyst. 14-16 In the past few decades, a great number of catalyst electronic structure modulation strategies have been reported, such as heteroatom doping, defect engineering, and surface and interface engineering, which can effectively enhance the electrocatalytic performance of catalysts. 12 For cobalt-based catalysts, due to the differences in physical properties such as ionic radius, valence and electronegativity between Co atoms and doping atoms, doping will lead to structural distortions and material defects in the catalyst, which can improve the inherent activity and structure of catalytically active substances. The coupling of doping elements and matrix materials can improve the interfacial properties of the catalyst, create more active sites and further improve the electrocatalytic efficiency.

China is the world's largest producer of rare earth elements, and its reserves and output rank first in the world. Rare earth elements have recently received particular attention for their ability to adjust electronic structures and improve the catalytic performance of various transition metal-based materials due to their distinct 4f subshell electrons and chemical characteristics. 17,18 Ce is a common rare earth material, and is mainly used in the field of catalytic materials, and the localized electrons in the 4f orbital can be stored and transported, and small molecules such as O2 can be adsorbed. Its common valence states are (III) and (IV), and it can be reversibly transformed between Ce(III) and Ce(IV). 19 When the doped Ce is coupled with other components, it shows a strong synergistic effect. In addition, the introduction of Ce also increases the stability and corrosion resistance of non-noble metal electrocatalytic materials during electrochemical testing.20 Therefore, the doping of rare earth element Ce is widely used in the field of electrochemistry. 21,22 Huang et al. prepared a rare earth Ce-doped NiFe layered double hydroxide (Ce-Ni-Fe-LDH) using a one-step method.<sup>23</sup> Thanks to the oxygen affinity and synergistic coordination of the decoration, the prepared Ce-Ni-Fe-LDH showed excellent catalytic activity for the alkaline OER. Besides, DFT theoretical calculations showed that the doping of rare earth Ce was helpful in stabilizing the initial morphology of the catalyst, optimizing the electronic structure of NiFe-LDH and adjusting the oxygencontaining intermediate on the catalyst surface. In addition, numerous studies have demonstrated that reactive oxygen species (ROS) derived from peroxides, as opposed to an H2O2 molecule, are responsible for the degradation of M-N-C catalysts.<sup>24</sup> However, Gubler et al. discovered that the Nafion polymer membranes can be shielded from hydroxyl radical degradation by a Ce3+/Ce4+ redox pair.25 In response to this, we considered doping a small quantity of Ce into Co to modify the surface electron distribution of the catalyst and boost its catalytic activity.

Folic acid (FA) can be carbonized at low temperature, with large supply and no pollution.<sup>26</sup> It is composed of a pteridine ring, p-aminobenzoic acid, and L-glutamic acid. It has a very high content of N elements and a large number of -COOH, -NH<sub>2</sub>, and -OH functional groups, which can coordinate with metal ions. The existence of metal complex ligand functional groups is very important for anchoring and improving the chemical stability of catalysts.

In this work, we propose a simple complexation strategy, using folic acid with a large number of -COOH and -NH2 groups to anchor metal ions. The NaCl template method is safe and easy to remove, which can increase the specific area of the catalyst and facilitate the formation of interconnected porous structures. Meanwhile, we used a low content of Ce<sup>3+</sup> promoter to modify the electronic state of the Co-based catalyst surface and to increase the intrinsic activity of the Co-based catalyst. Finally, N-doped carbon nanosheets Ce-Co/N/C were successfully synthesized and showed outstanding ORR activity. long-term durability, and great resistance to methanol toxicity.

# 2. Experimental section

#### 2.1. Materials synthesis

Synthesis of the Ce-Co-FA precursor. The synthesis method was as follows: under continuous agitation, 1 mmol of FA was put in a round-bottom flask filled with 30 mL ultra-pure water. The above solution was made neutral with 0.1 M NaOH. Subsequently, 0.95 mmol of CoCl<sub>2</sub>Â·6H<sub>2</sub>O and 0.05 mmol of Ce(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>Â·4H<sub>2</sub>O were then added to the solution. When the solution became uniform, the round-bottom flask was placed in an oil bath heating device and reacted at 60 Â°C for 30 min. The samples were centrifuged at a speed of 5000 rpm after they were cooled to room temperature, washed three or four times with anhydrous methanol, and dried for 10 hours in a vacuum drying oven at 65 Â°C.

Synthesis of Ce-Co/N/C. Typically, NaCl particles and Ce-Co-FA precursor were mixed at a mass ratio of 1:20 and then milled until the mixture was homogenized and then placed in a quartz boat. Next, the precursor was calcined with argon gas at 800 Â°C for 1 hour to obtain the sample. The sample was washed with plenty of ultrapure water to remove NaCl particles. After that, the sample was dried for 8 hours. The excess and disordered Co clusters were washed away by stirring with a 2 M HCl solution at 40 Â°C for 10 hours. The sample was neutralized with ultra-pure water and dried overnight in a 65 Â°C vacuum drying oven. The dried powder was placed in a tubular furnace filled with argon gas for secondary annealing at 800 Â°C. Ce-Co/N/C was obtained as the final catalyst. The same process was used to synthesize Co/N/C, N/C and Ce-Co/N/C-no NaCl.

#### 2.2. Material characterization

Transmission electron microscopy (TEM, model: JEM-2100F) was used to analyze the internal structure, thickness, and whether there were nanoparticles in the sample.<sup>27</sup> Solid X-ray diffraction (XRD, model: X' Pert PRO MPD) spectra were used to determine whether the sample was amorphous or crystalline, and at the same time, the phase composition of the analysis sample was determined by comparing with the standard card.<sup>28</sup> X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, model: ESCALAB250) was used to analyze the surface chemical composition of the final catalyst, and the valence state information of each component.29

And no obvious characteristic peaks of metals Ce and Co were observed in the Raman spectrum.

Utilizing nitrogen adsorption/desorption isotherms, the pore structures of all catalysts were identified. As shown in Fig. 3c, Ce-Co/N/C and Co/N/C catalysts displayed typical type IV isotherms and prominent H3-type hysteresis loops, revealing the presence of mesoporous structures. 39,40 In particular, in these catalysts, the hysteresis loop increased smoothly with an increase in  $P/P_0$ , which was generally considered to be a characteristic of a sheet-like structure. 41 These sheet-like structures can also be seen in the TEM images (Fig. 2). Co/N/C and Ce-Co/N/C both embodied numerous mesopores and micropores, and additionally quite a few macropores (Fig. 3d). The BET surface areas of the above three samples are summarized in Table S1 (ESIâ€ ). Compared with Co/N/C, the specific surface area of the Ce-Co/N/C catalyst was significantly increased to 442.47 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>-1</sup>, while the specific surface area of the Co/N/C catalyst was only 342.55 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>-1</sup>. Therefore, it is clear that the addition of Ce produced a porous catalyst with a high specific surface area. The reason may be that Ce doping caused more defects on carbon nanosheets, thus increasing the specific surface area of Ce-Co/N/C. The result is consistent with the Raman results. Also, NaCl as a template increases the surface area of the Ce-Co/N/C catalyst and promotes the formation of interconnected porous structures (Table S1, ESIâ€ ). In the ORR process, a porous structure and a large specific surface area can really increase the exposure probability of active sites, and also support the transport of ions or electrons, thus improving the catalytic activity.42

Combined with the above experimental results, the outstanding ORR catalytic activity of the Ce-Co/N/C catalyst can be attributed to the following factors. First, with the help of a sodium chloride template, Ce-Co/N/C forms a suitable pore structure with a large specific surface area. Second, the existence of Ce gives Ce-Co/N/C a higher defect degree and a higher percentage of total nitrogen content of graphite-N, Co-N and pyridine-N species, which makes the catalytic activity of ORR better. Finally, charge transfer occurs between Ce and Co, leading to a shift in Co 2p binding energy, which reflects a change in the electron cloud density of Co 2p, adjusting the adsorption strength of the ORR intermediate, which is favorable for the enhancement of catalytic performance of Ce-Co/N/C.

### 4. Conclusions

In conclusion, Ce-Co/N/C electrocatalysts were successfully prepared for fuel cell cathodic oxygen reduction by pyrolysis of the Ce-CoFA precursor assisted by a molten salt template, followed by removal of the template by acid etching of freely or loosely bound Co nanoparticles. The synthesized catalyst exhibited a thin twodimensional porous carbon nanosheet structure. Experimental studies showed that doping Ce<sup>3+</sup> into Co/N/C can significantly improve its catalytic activity and stability, and its performance and stability are better than those of commercial JM 20% Pt/C catalysts. Therefore, it will become possible in the future to replace precious metal catalysts for fuel cells with doped catalysts containing rare-earth elements.
